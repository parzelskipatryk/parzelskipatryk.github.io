Gramophone Puzzle Box

Puzzle Design Physical Prototyping Arduino

A physical escape-room puzzle built entirely from electrical components and an Arduino microcontroller — deliberately avoiding digital game engines as a design constraint. Players tune a dial, align a spinning record to hear correct audio, and operate a crank in the correct sequence to illuminate a series of lights. No screen. No software layer. Pure physical feedback.

The Constraint

The project brief challenged us to build a game experience using only physical electrical components — no Unity, no Unreal, no software to fall back on for feedback, state management, or instruction. Everything a player needed to understand had to be communicated through physical sensation: sound, light, resistance, and movement.

This constraint forced a kind of design rigour that digital projects often don't require. When you can't add a tooltip or a UI prompt to clarify an interaction, the interaction itself has to be self-explanatory.

Puzzle Design
  • Dial tuning: Players must rotate a physical dial — connected to a rotary encoder — to reach a correct value. Incorrect positions produce no positive feedback; the correct position triggers the next stage
  • Record alignment: A motor-driven spinning record must be aligned to a specific position for the gramophone's audio output to play correctly. Wrong alignment produces garbled or incorrect sound — the physical metaphor of a misaligned record doing exactly what a misaligned record would do
  • Crank sequencing: A hand crank must be turned in the correct direction a set number of times, triggering an LED sequence to illuminate in order. The light sequence provides feedback — partial completion is visible, wrong direction resets progress
  • Each stage's completion provides the information or access needed for the next — the puzzles are chained, not parallel
Affordance Design Without a Screen

The most interesting design challenge was communicating puzzle state and progress using only physical feedback. Every design decision had to answer the question: how does the player know what to do, and how do they know if they're succeeding?

  • Sound was the primary feedback channel for the dial and record stages — correct states produced clean audio, incorrect states produced degraded or absent audio
  • Light was used for the crank sequence — LEDs illuminating in order provided visible progress without needing any text or interface
  • Physical resistance and mechanical feel were used where possible to reinforce correct vs incorrect states — the crank clicks differently in the correct direction
  • The gramophone theme was chosen specifically because it justified the components narratively — a rotary encoder becomes a tuning dial, a motor becomes a record player. The narrative wrapper made the technical components feel intentional
