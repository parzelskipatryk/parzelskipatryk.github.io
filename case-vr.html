Relay

Level Design Puzzle Design Lighting Design VR Unity Asymmetric Co-op

A co-operative VR puzzle game built as a university group project. Two players share a physical space but occupy separate rooms with different information — they cannot see each other's puzzles, only communicate verbally. Every mechanic was designed around that constraint: what one player knows, the other needs. I led the level and puzzle design from initial blockout through to the final lit environment.

2Players
VRPlatform

Overview

The central design constraint for Relay was asymmetric information. Both players are physically present in the same VR space but separated into adjacent rooms. Neither player can see the other's puzzles. Progress requires constant verbal communication — one player describes what they see, the other acts on it.

Designing for this constraint meant every puzzle had to be built in pairs. Each mechanic on one side needed a corresponding role on the other. The challenge was making those roles feel balanced: both players needed to feel active and necessary, not one solving while the other watches.

Environment Showcase

The dungeon environment was built in a low-poly style with torch-based lighting to create atmosphere without heavy rendering cost in VR. Lighting was used deliberately to direct player attention toward puzzle elements and interaction points.

Spawn Room

The shared starting area where both players begin. A moonlight beam from above marks the central point before players split into their respective rooms. Designed to orient both players to the space before the asymmetric co-op begins.

Cave Environment

The main dungeon traversal area. Torch placement was used to create natural waypoints guiding players forward without explicit UI markers. The low-poly rock geometry keeps the VR frame rate stable while maintaining strong spatial readability.

Colour Communication Puzzle

Three skeletons hang in one player's room, each wearing a differently coloured accessory. The other player has a corresponding colour-coded wheel puzzle they need to solve. The catch: they cannot see the skeletons. They must ask their partner what colours they see and act on the answer. This is the core asymmetric information mechanic in its purest form.

Tile Navigation Puzzle

One player's room contains this black tile floor. Their partner holds a poster showing the exact same tile layout with the correct tiles marked in red. The partner must communicate the tile positions verbally while the player navigates. Wrong tiles trigger a reset. Designed to test spatial communication under pressure.

Gate and Lever Mechanic

A gate blocks progress in one room. The lever that opens it is on the other side of the wall — in the partner's room. One player must find and pull the lever while the other waits at the gate. A straightforward co-operation moment designed to reinforce the dependency between players early in the experience.

Gap Room

A spiked gap separates two platforms. Skulls hang from the ceiling above the gap. The player on the right side has a gun and must shoot each skull, causing platforms to rise temporarily on the left side. The left player times their crossing to the shots. Designed as the highest-tension co-operation moment in the level.

Battery Teleportation Pad (Left)

The left player carries a battery throughout the dungeon. Once they reach the top of the elevator, they place it on this pad to teleport it across to the right player. Without the battery, the right player cannot power the tile-revealing mechanism in their room — connecting the two halves of the dungeon's progression.

Battery Teleportation Pad (Right)

The receiving pad on the right player's side. Once the battery arrives, the right player uses it to illuminate the correct tiles in the tile navigation puzzle. The battery acts as the key that links both halves of the dungeon together — a physical object encoding the dependency between players.

Suspended Platform

Looking up at one of the chain-suspended platforms above the main cave, lit by torches to draw the eye upward and signal verticality as a navigable part of the space rather than just set dressing.

Puzzle Design

Each puzzle was designed as a pair: one side provides information or action, the other receives and responds. The puzzle sequence builds from simple verbal communication toward more complex interdependencies requiring precise timing and trust.

Puzzle 01
Colour Communication

Player A reads skeleton accessory colours verbally. Player B rotates a colour-coded wheel to match. Tests basic communication under no time pressure, establishing the verbal co-op language early.

Puzzle 02
Gate and Lever

One player pulls a lever in their room to open a gate in the other. Simple dependency mechanic that teaches players they cannot progress independently. Sets expectations for the rest of the level.

Puzzle 03
Gap Crossing

Player B shoots skulls to raise platforms while Player A times their crossing. First mechanic requiring real-time co-ordination rather than turn-taking communication. Highest tension moment by design.

Puzzle 04
Battery Relay

Player A carries a battery to the elevator top and teleports it to Player B, who uses it to reveal correct tiles. The battery physically connects both halves of the dungeon and pays off the asymmetric structure of the entire level.

Puzzle 05
Tile Navigation

Player A navigates a black tile floor. Player B holds the map showing which tiles are safe, marked in red, and must communicate the path verbally. Wrong tiles reset progress. Combines spatial description, memory, and trust — the most cognitively demanding puzzle, placed last as the final test of the communication system built up across the level.

Level Design

The level was designed blockout-first, with the spatial layout, room sizes, and connection points established before any assets were placed. In VR specifically, scale decisions made at blockout stage are very difficult to change later — getting the proportions right early was critical.

  • Designed the full level layout as a shared dungeon with two parallel paths, ensuring both players always felt they were in the same space without being able to see each other's area directly
  • Sized rooms to feel appropriately enclosed in VR without causing discomfort — tested at blockout stage with real headsets before committing to geometry
  • Designed the spatial progression so both players advance at roughly the same pace, preventing one player from finishing their side and waiting while the other catches up
  • Used the torch placement and lighting passes to create natural waypoints, directing players toward puzzle elements without any UI markers or arrows
  • Ran iterative playtesting at each stage of the build, observing where communication broke down and adjusting puzzle design or spatial layout accordingly
Lighting Design

All lighting in Relay was designed to serve two purposes simultaneously: atmosphere and navigation. In a VR experience without a HUD or minimap, lighting is the primary tool for directing player attention.

  • Used warm torch light to mark interaction points and puzzle elements, creating a visual hierarchy that drew players toward the correct objects
  • Kept ambient light cool and dim to ensure torch-lit areas read clearly and immediately as points of interest
  • Used the moonlight shaft in the spawn room as a spatial anchor, giving both players a shared reference point before they split into their separate areas
  • Tested lighting in headset throughout the build as VR light behaviour differs meaningfully from what appears in the Unity editor on a monitor
Result
First Class Relay was submitted as a university group project and received a First Class grade.
